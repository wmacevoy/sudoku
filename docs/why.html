Experience why paper Sudoku is better

Work through each section—notice how your thinking changes.

Step 1 of 6 · Easy (web, with hints)

Start

Open Sudoku with Help on. Play one easy game. Notice what your attention does.

Thinking about thinking
  • Did the hints teach you rules—or just move you forward?

After you finish

  • Did it feel like solving—or like pushing buttons?
  • Where was your attention most of the time?
This unlocks Step 2. Goal: feel the “mindless mode.”

Step 2 of 6 · Easy (paper, no hints)

Start

Disable help, generate an easy puzzle, print it, and solve it with pencil.

  • Did you look up the rules?
  • Where is your attention now?

Discussion prompt

  • Describe the difference in understanding, attention, and effort between web and paper.
  • Name one moment you nearly guessed. Why did—or didn’t—you stop?
This unlocks Step 3. Goal: feel the “workout mode.”

Step 3 of 6 · Easy (web again, with hints)

Start

Play another easy puzzle online with Help on.

What changed?

  • List two mechanics (e.g., scanning singles, box/row/column interactions) you noticed now that you missed in Step 1.
  • Are you still clicking mindlessly—or explaining to yourself why one option works and others do not?
This unlocks Step 4. Goal: experience the contrast.

Step 4 of 6 · Medium (web, with hints)

Start

Play one medium puzzle online.

Discussion prompts

  • What were you noticing this time? Where did hints help—and where did they ceiling out?
This unlocks Step 5. Goal: see the ceiling of assistance.

Step 5 of 6 · Medium (paper, no hints)

Start

Generate and print a medium puzzle. Solve it by hand. No assistance tools.

Debrief

  • How heavy was the mental load?
  • Were you disrupted by your phone or other distractions in mid-thought? Estimate your “context rebuild” cost per distraction and total it.
Unlocks the lesson. Goal: feel the full manual workout.

Step 6 of 6 · The Lesson

Start

Computers are elevators for your brain; useful, but if you never take the stairs, you go soft. LLMs raise the stakes: great tools, bad crutches. A paper Sudoku is a push-up for attention and logic. Do one; do it with a friend for social intelligence; invent twists for creativity. Your attention is under constant assault—protect it, train it, use it. Small practices compound; they shape how you think.

Tools like ChatGPT or GitHub Copilot are powerful—but if you let them think for you, your own muscles of logic, focus, and creativity atrophy. Just like elevators save steps but don’t build strength, LLMs can shortcut your effort but can bypass any understanding.

Every time you work a puzzle by hand, cook a meal from scratch, or explain your own solution out loud, you’re training the habits that make you resilient: focus under distraction, patience in problem-solving, and the confidence to trust your own reasoning. Those habits compound just like investing does—the earlier you start, the stronger they grow.

So here’s the shift: don’t use LLMs to replace your thinking. Use them the way you’d use a coach, a textbook, or a lab partner—to clarify rules, to check your work, to push your ideas further. First wrestle with the problem yourself; then bring in the tool to understand better or to test your solution.

Your future will be full of tools that make life easier. What will set you apart is not how fast you can copy an answer, but how deeply you understand and how boldly you can build your own. Protect your attention, train your mind, and keep practicing the “manual mode.” That is how you grow into someone who not only uses technology—but creates it.

Play Sudoku on paper (or some other offline mental workout). Cook a meal with friends. Play a social physical game. Practice mindfulness. These games compound into a healthy mind and healthy life.

You’ve unlocked your healthy-mind toolkit.
